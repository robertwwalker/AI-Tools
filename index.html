The Calculus of Chaos

Non-Stationary Shocks in Operations & Healthcare

1. The Theory of the "Shock"

In classical queueing, we assume the system is in Steady State ($ \rho < 1 $). However, real-world operations are often hit by Non-Stationary Shocks—sudden spikes where $ \lambda(t) \gg c\mu $.

$$\text{Accumulated Queue During Shock: } Q(t) = \int_{t_{start}}^{t} (\lambda(s) - c\mu) ds$$

When the shock ends, the system doesn't immediately return to normal. It must clear the "backlog." The **Recovery Time ($T_r$)** is the most critical metric for disaster planning:

$$T_r = \frac{Q(t_{end})}{c\mu - \lambda_{base}}$$

If your base utilization is already high, $ c\mu - \lambda_{base} $ is small, meaning a 1-hour shock could take 10 hours to recover from.

3. Structural Architecture

A. M/M/c & The Pooling Effect

Efficiency increases when servers are pooled. The probability of all servers being busy ($C(c, a)$) is given by the Erlang-C formula.

B. Circular (Finite Capacity)

In a **Circular Queue** with capacity $K$, the system enters a "Blocked" state when $n=K$. Any arrival during this time is lost (Hospital Diversion).

C. Priority (Triage Logic)

High-priority patients "cut" the line. This protects critical outcomes but creates **Variance Shocks** for low-priority arrivals, whose wait time distribution becomes "fat-tailed."

D. Deque (The Reneging Exit)

A **Double-Ended Queue** allows exits from the back. In healthcare, this models "Left Without Being Seen" (LWBS). Patients have a patience threshold; if $Wait > Tolerance$, they depart without service.
